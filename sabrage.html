The Art of Sabrage

While stories abound on the lineage of sabrage, Kevin’s favorite story revolves around the friendship between Jean-Remy Moët (pronounced MO –ET, not MO-AY), a French vintner and merchant seaman who sought to raise the house of Moët et Chandon to prominence.

In 1782, Jean-Rémy Moët met Napoleon Bonaparte at Brienne-le-Château, the military academy where Moët was gathering orders and soliciting business for his champagne firm. This was the beginning of a long-lasting friendship. Throughout his travels and campaigns, Napoleon relied on the Moët estate for his favorite champagne.

Legend has it that on an evening between battles, Napoleon tired of the tedium of traditional Champagne bottle opening techniques and instead unsheathed his saber and lopped off the neck of the nearest bottle at hand. ZAK! He taught his newly-discovered skill to his cavalry, the Hussars, and it quickly became a staple of post-victory celebrations in the field of battle. It is said that Napoleon was not able to return to the Moët estate at Épernay before the Battle of Waterloo to replenish his Champagne stocks. C’est la vie, c’est la guerre!
